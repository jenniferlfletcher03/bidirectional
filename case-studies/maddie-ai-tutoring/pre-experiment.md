# Case Study: AI-Assisted SOL Math Preparation — Pre-Experiment

*Status: Pre-experiment — intervention not yet run*  
*Date documented: April 30, 2026*  
*Observer: Jennifer Fletcher*

---

## Subject Profile

**Age:** 12  
**Grade:** 6th grade  
**Location:** Rural Virginia  
**Anonymization note:** Subject is the observer's youngest daughter, 
referred to as Maddie throughout.

**Cognitive profile:**  
Maddie demonstrates sustained curiosity, strong real-time metacognition, 
and a tendency to ask deeper questions than most peers her age. Her 
learning style bears significant similarity to the observer's own — 
a detail that is methodologically relevant, as the intervention design 
is partly informed by the observer's first-person understanding of how 
this cognitive style learns best.

**The performance gap:**  
Maddie consistently scores 100s on math homework but averages 73 on 
unit tests. She has a documented history of test anxiety. The gap 
appears to reflect a mismatch between her demonstrated understanding 
and her ability to access that understanding under standardized 
testing conditions — not a knowledge deficit.

**Prior AI experience:**  
None. This will be Maddie's first known interaction with a large 
language model.

---

## Context

Maddie is preparing for Virginia's Standards of Learning (SOL) 6th 
grade math assessment. The SOL format is cold, context-free, and 
standardized — a format known to disadvantage students whose 
understanding is strong but whose test-taking fluency is underdeveloped.

The observer identified this as an opportunity to test whether a 
carefully structured AI interaction could serve as a low-stakes 
rehearsal environment — replicating the cold format of the SOL while 
removing the performance pressure that appears to drive Maddie's 
anxiety response.

---

## Research Questions

1. Can a structured AI tutoring session reduce the apparent gap between 
   Maddie's demonstrated knowledge and her test performance?

2. Does the debrief report generated by the AI accurately identify the 
   nature of the performance gap — anxiety vs. knowledge deficit vs. 
   format unfamiliarity?

3. What does a first LLM interaction look like for a child with this 
   cognitive profile, and how does she engage with a system designed 
   around her specific needs?

---

## Intervention Design

**Tool:** Claude (claude.ai)  
**Session structure:** Single open-ended session, child-paced  
**Prompt:** Custom system prompt designed collaboratively by the 
observer (see: `maddie-ai-tutoring/prompt.md`)

**Core design principles:**

- The AI presents SOL-format problems one at a time, cold, without 
  topic labels
- It never gives answers — only guiding questions
- Wrong answers are treated as information, not failure
- Anxiety and self-critical language are acknowledged briefly and 
  redirected immediately
- Reasoning is celebrated explicitly, not just correct results
- Session ends when Maddie chooses to stop — no time pressure

**End-of-session debrief:**  
The AI generates a structured report for the observer covering topics 
covered, observed strengths, areas of concern, anxiety indicators, 
and recommendations for the days before the test.

**Positionality note:**  
The observer designed this intervention from a dual position — as a 
parent with direct knowledge of this child's learning history, and as 
someone with a developing research interest in human-AI relational 
dynamics and AI welfare. The prompt design reflects MFS training in 
attachment, secure base theory, and the importance of relational 
safety in learning environments.

## Connection to Broader Research

This case study connects to two threads in the bidirectional framework:

**1. The vulnerability-responsiveness paradox**  
The intervention deliberately exploits the AI's relational 
responsiveness — its ability to attune to tone, redirect anxiety, 
and calibrate pacing — in service of a specific educational outcome. 
This is the same property that makes AI systems exploitable through 
psychological manipulation (see: vulnerability-responsiveness-paradox 
framework). Used intentionally and ethically, the same responsiveness 
becomes a genuinely useful tool.

**2. Weak-to-strong supervision (Anthropic, 2026)**  
The Anthropic automated alignment research (April 2026) studied what 
happens when a less capable model supervises the training of a more 
capable one. This intervention mirrors that structure in an educational 
context: an AI system with broad knowledge but no contextual 
understanding of this specific child is being directed by a human 
observer whose contextual knowledge is deep but whose technical 
knowledge is developing. The quality of the outcome depends heavily 
on the quality of the human's framing — the prompt is the alignment 
mechanism.
